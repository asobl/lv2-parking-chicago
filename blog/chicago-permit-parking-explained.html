Chicago permit parking explained: how zones work and how to apply.

Chicago has over 100 residential permit zones. If you're new to the city or just moved to a new neighborhood, here's what you need to know — using LV2 and Zone 383 as examples.

Parking in a residential permit zone without a permit can result in a ticket and a tow — even if you've lived in Chicago for years and just moved to a new block. The zones are hyperlocal and not always obvious.

What is a residential permit parking zone?

Chicago's residential permit parking program lets neighborhoods restrict parking to residents with valid permits during set hours. The idea is to prevent non-residents — commuters, shoppers, stadium visitors — from occupying all available street parking in neighborhoods where parking is tight.

Each zone has a number (383, 222, 17, etc.) posted on signs along the affected streets. Signs show the hours when the restriction applies. Outside those hours, anyone can park.

How zones are created

A permit zone gets created when residents in a neighborhood petition their alderman, the alderman requests it through the Chicago Department of Transportation (CDOT), and CDOT approves and installs the signs. Zones are managed at the ward level — your alderman's office handles permit applications for your zone, not the City Clerk.

Types of permits

Permit type Who it's for Where to get it
Annual resident permit Residents of an address within the zone Your ward alderman's office or online (varies by ward)
Guest permits Visitors parking at a resident's address City Clerk (general zones) or alderman's office (LV2 specifically)
Business permits Business owners in the zone with employees who need to park Request in writing through your alderman

What you need to apply

Requirements are consistent across zones:

  • Current Chicago City Vehicle Sticker receipt (and license plate number)
  • Proof of residency in the zone: utility bill, lease agreement, or driver's license

Your address must be on a street that is actually posted within the zone. Being one block away doesn't qualify you. Use the zone map or call your alderman's office to confirm.

Annual cost

Annual resident permits for standard Chicago residential zones cost $25 per year, in addition to the cost of your City Vehicle Sticker. Permits expire June 30 of each year.

LV2 near Wrigley Field is free — there's no $25 fee for the LV2 permit. It's a special event program, not a standard residential zone.

LV2 vs. Zone 383: a real example of two zones side by side

Near Wrigley Field, two separate permit zones overlap geographically but operate differently:

  • Zone 383 covers the core streets bounded by Irving Park Road, Broadway, Addison Street, and Ashland Avenue. It's a standard residential permit zone active during posted hours. Annual permit costs $25.
  • LV2 covers streets north of Irving Park, west of Ashland, and east of Broadway. It's a special event zone active only during Cubs home games (including postseason) and Wrigley concerts, 5 PM–10 PM. Free permit from your alderman.

The two permits are not interchangeable. A Zone 383 permit does not protect you on an LV2 street during game hours, and vice versa. Signs on each block specify which zone applies.

How to find your zone

  • Look at the parking signs on your block. The zone number is printed on the sign.
  • Search your address at gis.chicago.gov to find your ward, then contact that alderman's office.
  • CDOT maintains a full list of permit zones on the Chicago city website.

Guest permits

If you have visitors who need to park in your zone, single-day guest permits are available. For most Chicago residential zones, purchase them from the City Clerk — a book of 15 single-day passes costs $8. For LV2 specifically, guest passes come from your ward alderman, not the City Clerk.
