Red Line vs. driving to Wrigley: an honest comparison.

The right answer depends on where you're coming from and how many people are in your group. Here's the actual math.

The Red Line wins on cost and stress for solo trips or groups of two from anywhere on the north side. Driving wins for groups of four coming from the suburbs where there's no reasonable transit option.

The cost math

Option Solo Group of 4
Red Line (round trip) ~$5 (2 fares × $2.50) ~$20 (8 fares)
Garage parking (SpotHero) $20–$45 depending on day/time Same $20–$45, split 4 ways
Street parking outside LV2 Free if you find it (walk 15–25 min) Free if you find it
Cubs remote lot shuttle Free parking + free shuttle (baseball games only) Same — great value for groups

For a group of four, driving and splitting a parking spot often costs $5–$11 per person — competitive with transit. For solo or two people, the Red Line is almost always cheaper.

The time math

This depends entirely on where you're starting:

  • From the Loop: Red Line to Addison is about 25 minutes, no traffic stress. Driving the same route on game day can take 40–60 minutes with congestion on Clark or Broadway.
  • From Evanston/Rogers Park: Red Line wins — direct shot south. Driving means navigating toward Wrigley against traffic on a game day.
  • From the south suburbs (Naperville, Orland Park, etc.): No good transit option. Drive to a Metra station if you can, or drive most of the way and park at the remote lot.
  • From the north suburbs (Wilmette, Glenview): Metra UP-N line to Ogilvie, then Red Line north. Takes longer but avoids parking costs entirely.

The stress factor

This is real and worth pricing in. On a sold-out game, parking near Wrigley is a 20–45 minute project even if you have a reservation. After the game, you're sitting in post-game traffic for another 30–45 minutes to get out of the neighborhood. That's an hour of your night, every game.

The Red Line drops you at Addison and Clark, one block from the gate. After the game, you're on the train and moving within 10 minutes. The train is crowded, but it's moving.

The verdict: Take the Red Line if you're coming from anywhere on the north side or the Loop, and there are 1–2 of you. Drive (and use the remote shuttle or a reserved garage) if you're a group of 4+ or coming from somewhere without reasonable transit access.

When driving makes sense

  • Group of 4 splitting parking costs
  • Bad weather (cold, rain — especially after a game at night)
  • Coming from suburbs with no direct transit
  • Day game where you want to leave immediately after
  • Accessibility needs that make transit difficult

When the Red Line wins

  • Solo or pair coming from the north side or Loop
  • Night game (easier post-game departure)
  • You want to drink without worrying about it
  • Weeknight game where parking is aggressive and expensive
  • You don't have a parking reservation and are hoping to find street parking

The remote lot option (best of both worlds)

For baseball games, the Cubs run a free shuttle from a remote parking lot at 4650 N. Clarendon Ave. You get free parking and a free shuttle — a 10-minute ride to the park. It fills up, so arrive early. More details in our shuttle guide.
