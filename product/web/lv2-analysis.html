Data Analysis

What the FOIA ticket data says

28%
One street accounts for more than a quarter of all LV2 tickets
N Marshfield Ave racked up 2,269 of 8,046 mapped tickets between 2018 and 2023. Add in N Paulina St and two streets alone account for 44.6% of every ticket written in the entire zone. The rest of the 23 streets share what’s left. If you park on Marshfield on a game night, you are not just parking in the zone — you are parking in the enforcement zone.
1%
26 blocks generate the most heat. The rest is spread thin.
Of 2,607 geocoded address points in the LV2 zone, just 26 (1%) crossed the 15-ticket threshold over five years. The top 10 individual blocks combined for only 225 tickets, 2.8% of all mapped violations. Enforcement is real but diffuse. There is no single block where you are almost certain to get a ticket. There is one street (Marshfield) where you are significantly more likely to.
14.7%
Nearly 1 in 7 tickets can’t be placed on a map
Of 9,434 LV2 tickets in the FOIA dataset, 1,388 couldn’t be geocoded, addresses on diagonal streets like Clark and Lincoln Ave, or streets not in the lookup table. Those 1,388 tickets represent real enforcement on real blocks that fall outside the grid math the Chicago address system relies on. Any analysis using only the mapped data is working with 85% of the picture. Diagonal streets near Wrigley carry heavy foot and car traffic on game days, and officers work them.
Source: Chicago FOIA request (LV2 tickets 2018-2023)

Data Analysis

What the camera data says

−31%
The Irving Park speed camera actually slows down on game days
In 2025, the CHI095 speed camera at 1142 W Irving Park averaged 109 violations on non-game days but only 74 on Cubs home game days. Speed cameras in Chicago are school zone cameras, active during school hours. Cubs games skew heavily toward nights and weekends, when the camera is in low-enforcement mode anyway. The game isn’t changing anyone’s driving. The school bell is.
+23%
The Clark & Irving Park red light sees a real game-day spike
Red light cameras at Clark and Irving Park tell a different story. On Cubs home game days in 2025, the two cameras at that intersection averaged 9.7 combined violations per day versus 7.9 on non-game days, a 23% lift. The peak was August 17, 2025, a Sunday day game against the Cardinals: 21 violations at that intersection alone. More cars coming and going from Wrigley means more people pushing the yellow.

Data Analysis

What the game factors and weather tell you

4x
Big crowds generate four times the red light violations of small crowds
On days when Cubs attendance tops 40,000, red light cameras at Clark and Irving average 18.1 violations. On small-crowd days under 30k, that drops to 4.5. The scatter plot makes this visible: violations cluster higher as attendance climbs.
+52%
Hot game days produce 52% more red light violations than cold ones
Games above 80F average 17.3 red light violations vs 11.4 on cold days below 60F. Temperature predicts violations better than whether it’s a rivalry game or a big series. When it’s hot, people drive. When it’s cold, fewer do, and those who do are more cautious.
−22%
Rain cuts red light violations by 22%
Game days with more than 0.1 inches of rain average 12.2 red light violations vs 15.7 on dry days. Rain reduces driving, reduces speed, and signals to people that walking or transit might be a better call. The intersection still gets busy, just less recklessly so.
No effect
40,000 Cubs fans do not move the needle on air quality
Cook County AQI averages 64.3 on Cubs home game days and 65.6 on non-game days, essentially identical. Air quality is driven by regional weather patterns, industrial sources, and traffic across the whole metro area. One stadium’s worth of cars, spread over a few hours, is lost in the noise at county scale.

Data Analysis

What 12 years of camera data tells you

4× overnight
Chicago changed one number and the speed camera exploded
In March 2021 Chicago lowered the speed camera enforcement threshold from 10 mph over the limit to 6 mph over. The CHI095 camera at Irving Park went from 19,064 violations in 2019 to 75,077 in 2021 — nearly 4x. This is not a traffic behavior change. It is a policy change that created a revenue spike on a street that runs directly past a school and a major league ballpark.
−52%
Drivers adapted — speed cam violations have fallen every year since 2021
From the 2021 peak of 75,077 violations, CHI095 has declined every year: 57,283 (2022), 43,867 (2023), 37,249 (2024), 35,824 (2025, partial). That is a 52% drop in four years. Drivers near Wrigley have learned where the camera is and adjusted. Whether that is genuine slower driving or just foot-off-the-gas at that specific block is a different question.
4× growth
The Ashland & Irving red light tells a slower, steadier growth story
While the speed camera spiked and crashed, the Ashland and Irving red light cameras grew steadily from 721 violations in 2014 to a peak of 2,850 in 2023 — nearly a 4x increase over nine years. This intersection handles traffic arriving from the west on Irving Park and turning south on Ashland, a route heavily used by drivers leaving Cubs games. The growth tracks the general increase in post-game traffic and rideshare volume over that period.
2020 dip
COVID shows up clearly — fewer games, fewer cars, fewer violations
The 2020 Cubs season ran 30 home games instead of 81, all without fans. The Clark and Irving red light camera dropped to 2,115 violations — the lowest since 2015. The Ashland camera dipped to 1,577. The speed camera did not drop as sharply (25,309 in 2020) because Irving Park carries year-round traffic independent of Cubs games. The red lights are more game-sensitive. The speed camera catches everyone, game day or not.
Source: Chicago Data Portal speed camera violations (hhkd-xvj4) and red light camera violations (spqx-js37), 2014-2025. 2025 data through approximately April 2026 publication.
