Wrigley Field off-season events: does LV2 still apply?

LV2 does not activate for concerts, college football games, winter events, or any non-Cubs activity at Wrigley. The ordinance specifies Cubs home games only. Other restrictions often still apply. Here's how to tell the difference.

Published November 12, 2026

LV2 is tied specifically to Cubs home games. For concerts, college football, winter ice skating, corporate events, and any other non-Cubs activity at Wrigley, LV2 does not activate. Cars without LV2 permits can park legally on LV2-only blocks during these events.

The ordinance is specific

Chicago Municipal Code 9-68-023 ties LV2 enforcement to "days a Cubs home game is played at Wrigley Field." The language does not include concerts. It does not include college football. It does not include any non-baseball activity. The zone activates only when the Cubs are the home team of a scheduled MLB game.

This is intentional. LV2 was created to address the parking pressure from 80-plus Cubs home games per year. Other events at Wrigley, while disruptive in their own way, happen only a handful of times per year and the city handles them through event-specific temporary restrictions rather than a permanent zone.

What does activate for non-Cubs events

While LV2 does not apply, the city frequently issues event-specific temporary restrictions for high-attendance events at Wrigley. These work differently from LV2:

  • Temporary no-parking signs are posted by Streets and Sanitation at least 72 hours in advance of the event. The signs cover specific blocks for a specific date and time. They look like small orange or red paper signs stapled to wooden stakes along the curb.
  • Pedestrian perimeter closures are announced by Chicago Police for concerts and major events. These close Clark, Addison, Waveland, or Sheffield to vehicle traffic during event ingress and egress windows.
  • Rideshare pickup zones are set up with temporary signage to manage Uber and Lyft flow. Private vehicles parked in these zones are ticketed.
  • Tow-zone activation can be applied to key streets for high-security events. The signs specify the activation window. Cars left in the activated zone are towed.
The temporary signs matter more than the permanent signs during non-Cubs events. A block that is normally legal parking can become a no-parking zone for a specific concert or football game, announced 72 hours before the event. Always re-read the signs before leaving your car during off-season event days.

Residential permit zones stay active

The residential permit zones in the Wrigley area operate independently of LV2. A block that is in a standard residential permit zone requires a permit all year, including during Cubs off-season and during non-Cubs events at Wrigley.

This catches visitors. Someone attending a July concert at Wrigley might assume the neighborhood is "open" because it's not a Cubs game day. Many of the blocks around the ballpark are covered by 24/7 residential permit zones that are unaffected by LV2 status.

Specific event patterns

Summer concerts

Concerts at Wrigley draw 30,000 to 40,000 fans, comparable to a sold-out Cubs game. The city typically posts temporary no-parking signs on key blocks (Waveland, Sheffield, Clark near the ballpark) for concert days. LV2 is not active, but the temporary signs and pedestrian closures create a similar parking experience for visitors.

Notre Dame football

The rare Notre Dame home game at Wrigley brings a different kind of disruption. Tailgate setups, nationally-televised production trucks, and the overall crowd intensity produce a heavier restriction pattern than a Cubs game. Expect wider-area no-parking signs, extended pedestrian perimeter, and heavy CPD presence.

Winterland

The annual ice skating rink operates from mid-November through February. Daily open-skate hours produce minimal parking impact. Tournament days and weekend hockey events can trigger localized temporary restrictions. The rink itself is inside the ballpark concourse, so the restrictions are less extensive than a full-stadium event.

Private events

Corporate buyouts, weddings, and private parties at Wrigley range from small (minimal street impact) to large (temporary no-parking signs on adjacent blocks). The city does not publish a comprehensive list of private events. Always check the curb for freshly posted signs before leaving your car.

The bottom line for off-season events

  • LV2 is dormant on any day there is no Cubs home game
  • Standard residential permit zones still apply year-round
  • Temporary event-specific no-parking signs override everything when posted
  • Read the curb signs every time, even on familiar blocks
  • For concerts and high-attendance non-Cubs events, expect a restricted environment similar to a Cubs game day even without LV2

If you are uncertain whether a given date has a Cubs home game, check the LV2 Park homepage. It shows the Cubs home schedule and whether LV2 is active that day.
