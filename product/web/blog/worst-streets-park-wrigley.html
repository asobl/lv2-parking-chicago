The worst streets to park near Wrigley Field, ranked by ticket volume

Five years of FOIA data, one uncomfortable answer: enforcement in the LV2 zone is not spread evenly. It concentrates hard on a handful of streets, and one street above all others.

Published July 16, 2026  ·  FOIA data 2018-2023

N Marshfield Ave collected 2,269 of 8,046 mapped LV2 tickets between 2018 and 2023. That is 28% of every ticket written in the entire zone. On a single street.

We submitted a FOIA request to the City of Chicago and received 9,434 LV2 parking tickets covering 2018 to 2023. When you map those tickets by street, the picture is not what most people expect. The enforcement weight is not spread across the zone. It stacks on specific streets and specific blocks, and one street carries nearly a third of the load by itself.

If you are deciding where to park on a Cubs game night, this data should change your answer.

Two streets, Marshfield and Paulina, account for 44.6% of every mapped LV2 ticket over five years. Add Hermitage and Roscoe and four streets account for two-thirds of all enforcement. The remaining 19 streets in the dataset share the last third.

The Marshfield situation

Marshfield is a north-south residential street that runs parallel to Paulina and Ravenswood on the west side of the LV2 zone. It is not a commercial street. It has no bars or restaurants drawing foot traffic. It is just a residential street that happens to sit in the zone and happens to be where officers concentrate their enforcement.

The hottest single block in the entire dataset is 3406 N Marshfield Ave with 45 tickets over five years. The second hottest is 1616 W Roscoe St at 26. The drop-off is steep: the top block has nearly twice the tickets of the second block.

Why Marshfield specifically? A few likely reasons. It is a longer uninterrupted stretch within the zone, which makes it efficient to work. It has consistent on-street parking without the interruptions of driveways and loading zones that break up other streets. And based on the ticket density, officers appear to stage there deliberately as part of their enforcement pattern.

The blocks of Marshfield around 3400-3445 N appear repeatedly in the top 30 hotspot list. If you are parking on Marshfield anywhere in that stretch on a game night, you are parking in the highest-concentration enforcement area in the entire zone.

What about the streets closer to Wrigley

Sheffield, Clark, Waveland, and Addison, the streets immediately adjacent to the stadium, barely appear in the ticket data at all. W Addison St has 4 tickets in the entire five-year dataset. W Waveland Ave has 222, but much of that is probably concentrated in the blocks nearest the park.

This makes sense. Those streets are commercial corridors with meters, loading zones, and bus stops. The LV2 zone's residential restrictions apply to the residential streets behind them, not to the commercial strips out front. Officers are not wasting time on Sheffield Avenue, where cars either have paid meter time or are in a loading zone. They are working the side streets where residential restrictions are clear and enforcement is fast.

The unmapped 15%

About 1,388 of the 9,434 tickets in the FOIA dataset could not be geocoded. Most of these are on diagonal streets: Clark, Lincoln, and Elston, which cut through the grid and confuse the address lookup system. Those streets carry real game-day traffic and real enforcement. The mapped data is 85% of the picture.

N Clark St in particular is a likely high-enforcement street based on observation, but the diagonal address system means those tickets don't geocode cleanly. Take the ranked table above as a guide to the grid streets, knowing that diagonal street enforcement is an additional layer on top of it.

What this means for your parking decision

The data does not say that parking on Paulina or Roscoe is safe. The zone is the zone. Every car parked in the LV2 zone without a valid permit after 5 PM on a game day is subject to a ticket and tow. The data says that if you are parking in the zone at all, Marshfield is where you are most likely to find officers working first.

The clearest takeaway is directional: park further from Marshfield if you have a choice between two equally valid spots. But the safest answer remains the same one it has always been: get out of the zone entirely, or have a valid permit.
