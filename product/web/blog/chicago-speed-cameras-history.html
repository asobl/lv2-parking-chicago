Chicago speed camera rules: how they've changed and what's coming.

The program has existed for over a decade. Its rules have changed meaningfully only once. That one change, in 2021, produced one of the largest single-year shifts in Chicago traffic enforcement in a generation.

Published December 10, 2026

Chicago's speed camera program launched in 2013 with a 10 mph threshold for fines. In March 2021, the threshold dropped to 6 mph. That single policy change quadrupled violations at some cameras within a year and generated hundreds of millions in new revenue. Every other rule of the program has stayed substantially the same since 2014.

The legislative foundation

Chicago's speed cameras operate under authority granted by the Illinois Automated Speed Enforcement Act. The state law specifically restricts automated speed enforcement to "safety zones" near schools and parks. The cameras cannot be placed on arbitrary roads. They can only enforce in zones the law defines.

A school safety zone extends one-eighth of a mile from a school boundary. A park safety zone extends one-eighth of a mile from a park boundary. The zones are fixed and mapped by ordinance. Cameras placed outside these zones are not authorized to issue citations.

The timeline

2003
Illinois Automated Traffic Law Enforcement Act enables municipalities to use cameras for red light enforcement. Chicago begins red light camera deployment shortly after.
2011
Illinois passes specific legislation authorizing automated speed enforcement in Chicago's school and park safety zones. The statute is narrower than the red light camera program and imposes the school/park zone restriction.
2013
Chicago launches the speed camera program under Mayor Rahm Emanuel. Initial deployment is limited, with warnings issued for the first 30 days after each camera's installation and a 10 mph threshold for fines. Speeds 6-10 mph over produce warnings. Speeds 11 mph and higher produce fines.
2014
Program expands significantly. Cameras reach approximately 150 locations across the city. The basic structure (10 mph threshold, warnings only below that) holds. Revenue exceeds $50 million annually.
2015 - 2020
Program operates with relatively stable rules. Fines are $35 for 6-10 mph over (after the warning period) and $100 for 11+ mph over, BUT this was a warnings-only range for most of this period at most cameras. The official threshold at which money changes hands for most violations stays at 11 mph.
March 2021
Mayor Lori Lightfoot's administration lowers the fine threshold from 10 mph over to 6 mph over. The change takes effect March 1, 2021. Any driver exceeding the limit by 6 mph or more now receives a $35 fine. The first violation of the year is still a warning. Every violation after that produces a ticket.
2021 (remainder)
Violation volumes spike at virtually every active camera. Some cameras go from a few thousand violations per year to tens of thousands. Revenue more than doubles in one year. Public backlash begins immediately, with debates in the City Council and media coverage highlighting regressive impact and enforcement concentration in specific neighborhoods.
2022 - 2023
City Council takes up the 6 mph threshold in multiple sessions. Proposals to return to 10 mph fail to pass. The threshold remains at 6 mph. Camera deployment continues with incremental additions near newly designated school zones.
2024 - 2025
Program stabilizes at the post-2021 baseline. Violation volumes remain elevated compared to pre-2021 levels. The 6 mph threshold continues. Mayor Brandon Johnson's administration inherits the program without material changes to thresholds or fines.
2026
Current state. 6 mph threshold, $35 fine for 6-10 over, $100 for 11+ over, 6 AM to 11 PM enforcement hours in safety zones.

The impact of the 2021 change

The single most consequential change in the program's history was lowering the threshold from 10 mph to 6 mph. At the camera near Wrigley Field at 1142 W. Irving Park, annual violations went from roughly 19,000 in 2020 to 75,000 in 2022. The physical camera did not change. The speed of the traffic did not change significantly. The definition of "violation" changed, and the enforcement captured three times as many events as a result.

We covered the Wrigley-specific impact in detail in our speed camera 2021 post. The pattern was consistent across the city: cameras in areas with light to moderate pre-2021 traffic saw the largest relative jumps, because they were capturing a previously-untouched slice of drivers who exceeded the limit by 6-9 mph.

The 6 mph threshold is a pure policy choice. The cameras were physically capable of detecting 6-10 mph violations the entire time. The decision about whether to convert those detections into fines is a single line in city code.

Current rules in effect

  • Threshold: 6 mph over the posted limit
  • First violation per year: warning
  • Subsequent 6-10 mph over: $35 fine
  • Subsequent 11+ mph over: $100 fine
  • Hours: 6 AM to 11 PM (24 hours in some park zones)
  • Location restriction: only within school or park safety zones
  • Warning period: first 30 days after a camera is installed, warnings only
  • Payment: $35 fine due in 25 days; doubles to $70 if unpaid
  • Contest: online at cityofchicago.org, by mail, or at the Department of Administrative Hearings

Where the program may go next

Three policy directions have been floated in recent City Council and community discussions:

Returning the threshold to 10 mph

Multiple proposals have been introduced since 2022 to restore the 10 mph threshold. None have passed. The revenue implications make rollback politically difficult even when individual council members support it.

Expanding the program to additional camera locations

The city has added cameras near newly designated school zones at a modest pace. Expansion beyond the school/park zone restriction would require state legislative action.

Graduated fine structure

Some proposals suggest scaling fines based on a driver's income, following models used in Nordic countries. This is unlikely to pass in Illinois without significant legal challenges, but the conversation continues.

What drivers near Wrigley should know

Three cameras actively enforce near Wrigley Field: the school zone camera at 1142 W. Irving Park, and red light cameras at Clark and Irving Park and at Ashland and Irving Park. The speed camera operates under the 6 mph threshold rule. The red light cameras enforce on a separate system, with a $100 fine for running a red or failing to stop before turning right.

On Cubs game days, traffic around Wrigley is dense and the cameras are active. The combination of post-game congestion and aggressive driver behavior produces elevated violation volumes, especially at the Clark and Irving Park intersection.
