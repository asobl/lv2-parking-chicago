How rideshare changed game-day traffic near Wrigley Field

The Ashland and Irving red light camera grew from 721 violations in 2014 to 2,850 in 2023, nearly a 4x increase over nine years. No policy change. No new enforcement. The intersection just got steadily busier. Here's why.

Published September 24, 2026  ·  Camera data 2014-2025

The Clark and Irving red light camera, which captures traffic heading north on Clark after Cubs games, stayed relatively stable from 2014 to 2023. The Ashland and Irving camera, which captures traffic heading south and west, grew 4x. The difference is in who uses each route and how that changed as rideshare became the dominant post-game transport choice for a large share of fans.

We pulled annual violation totals for both intersections from 2014 through 2025. The divergence between the two cameras is one of the more interesting patterns in the decade of data.

Clark and Irving is the busier of the two intersections overall, sitting directly on Clark Street north of the park. But Ashland and Irving, about six blocks west, grew faster on a percentage basis from 2014 to its 2023 peak. By 2023, the two cameras were recording nearly identical annual violation counts despite Ashland/Irving being a less central intersection.

The rideshare hypothesis

Uber launched in Chicago in 2011. Lyft launched in 2012. By the 2014 Cubs season, rideshare was available but not yet dominant for post-game pickup. By 2018 it was common. By 2022 it was the default transportation choice for a large share of Cubs fans who were not taking the Red Line.

The key geography: Ashland Avenue is one of the designated rideshare pickup corridors near Wrigley Field. Because the immediate blocks around the stadium are congested with pedestrian traffic and are often designated no-stopping zones on game days, the pickup areas for Uber and Lyft have been pushed to the surrounding arterials. Ashland, running south from Irving Park, became a natural staging and pickup corridor as rideshare volume grew.

More rideshare drivers on Ashland means more cars making turns on Irving Park from Ashland heading toward the highway, more cars double-parked or slowing traffic waiting for pickups, and more drivers making marginal timing decisions at the Ashland/Irving light. The camera data captures the aggregate result of all of that.

Why Clark/Irving did not grow the same way

Clark Street is already a high-volume commercial corridor. Its 2014 baseline was higher, and additional vehicle traffic from rideshare blends into an already busy street pattern. The marginal impact of more rideshare vehicles on Clark is smaller because Clark was already handling high traffic volumes from bars, restaurants, and through-traffic.

Ashland, by contrast, was primarily a residential and local-traffic arterial in 2014. The addition of rideshare pickup volume to that corridor represented a proportionally larger change to how the street was being used.

What it means for parking near Wrigley today

If you are parking west of Ravenswood and walking to Wrigley, you are parking in territory that has gotten meaningfully busier in the post-game window since 2014. The streets between Ravenswood and Ashland carry both fan foot traffic and rideshare vehicle traffic in the 30-60 minutes after a game ends. Plan your exit accordingly, and treat Ashland itself as a congested arterial post-game rather than a quick escape route.
