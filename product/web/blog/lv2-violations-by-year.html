LV2 violations by year: how enforcement has changed since 2018

A full accounting of what the cameras and FOIA data show across more than a decade of enforcement near Wrigley Field. Policy changes, COVID, driver adaptation, and the 2021 speed camera spike all show up clearly in the numbers.

Published October 22, 2026  ·  Camera data 2014-2025, FOIA data 2018-2023

Three distinct forces shaped the numbers near Wrigley over the past decade: a policy change in 2021 that instantly quadrupled speed camera violations, a COVID year that compressed the Cubs season to 30 home games without fans, and a gradual multi-year increase in red light violations driven by more traffic and more rideshare vehicles in the neighborhood.

We built the most complete year-over-year picture of parking and traffic enforcement near Wrigley Field that has ever been published. Here is what each data source shows across time.

Speed camera CHI095 at 1142 W Irving Park

The speed camera on Irving Park east of Wrigley is a school zone camera active during school hours. Its violations track all-day Irving Park traffic, not specifically game-day traffic. The 2021 spike is entirely a policy story: Chicago lowered the enforcement threshold from 10 mph over the speed limit to 6 mph over in March 2021, and violations nearly quadrupled overnight.

Red light cameras at Clark/Irving and Ashland/Irving

The red light cameras tell a different story: steady growth with no single policy cliff, a COVID dip in 2020, and a 2024 record high at Clark/Irving. These cameras capture game-day post-game traffic more directly than the school-hours speed camera.

Clark/Irving hit its all-time record of 3,423 violations in 2024. Ashland/Irving peaked in 2023 at 2,850. Both cameras are well above their 2014 baselines, reflecting a decade of growth in overall traffic volume and rideshare pickup activity in the Wrigley area.

LV2 parking tickets from FOIA (2018-2023)

The FOIA dataset of 9,434 LV2 parking tickets covers a different enforcement layer: the residential zone itself. The ticket data does not break down by year in a way that shows clean annual trends, but it establishes the five-year enforcement footprint across the zone. Marshfield and Paulina carry the most tickets. The top 4 streets account for two-thirds of all mapped violations.

The 2020 COVID year is visible in the camera data. The Clark/Irving red light camera dropped from 2,747 (2018) to 2,115 (2020). The Cubs played 30 home games with no fans. Less post-game traffic, fewer cars at the intersection, fewer violations. The speed camera barely moved because it captures all-day school-zone traffic that continued regardless of Cubs games.

The overall trajectory

Speed camera violations near Wrigley: down sharply from the 2021 policy spike, likely to stabilize in the 30,000-40,000 range as driver adaptation levels off. Red light violations: gradually increasing over the long term, with short-term variation driven by season schedule, attendance, and weather. LV2 ticket enforcement: steady and concentrated, with no indication from the data that enforcement intensity has changed meaningfully year over year within the FOIA period.
