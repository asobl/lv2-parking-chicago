Cubs sold-out game parking: what the camera data says to expect

Attendance is the single strongest predictor of traffic violations near Wrigley. The difference between a small crowd and a sellout is not incremental. It is fourfold.

Published July 23, 2026  ·  Camera data 2025 Cubs season

On Cubs home game days with attendance above 40,000, red light cameras at Clark and Irving averaged 18.1 violations per day. On days with attendance below 30,000, the average was 4.5. That is a 4x difference driven entirely by how many people showed up.

We cross-referenced every 2025 Cubs home game against daily red light camera violations at two intersections near Wrigley Field: Clark and Irving Park, and Ashland and Irving Park. Then we matched each game against the official MLB attendance figure. The crowd size effect on traffic behavior is the clearest pattern in the data.

The jump from small-crowd to big-crowd is 4x. The jump from non-game day to big-crowd game is also substantial. More cars driving to and from the stadium means more congestion, more impatience, and more people making the push through a yellow that becomes a red.

The single highest-violation day

The peak day in the 2025 dataset was August 17, a Sunday day game against the St. Louis Cardinals. Attendance was over 40,000 and the day game timing put the post-game rush directly into the late-afternoon traffic window on Irving Park. The Clark and Irving cameras recorded 21 combined violations that day, the highest single-day total in the Cubs home season.

Cardinals series games and Cubs-Sox games tend to draw some of the largest and most aggressive crowds. The data reflects that.

Why the Clark and Irving intersection specifically

Clark and Irving Park is a major intersection directly in the path of post-game traffic heading north and west from Wrigley. Fans leaving on foot, rideshares, and cars all funnel through or near this intersection. The cameras at 1091 and 1093 capture both directions of traffic on Clark at Irving.

Ashland and Irving, about six blocks west, captures a different flow: fans who drove, parked west of the Ravenswood corridor, and are heading south on Ashland post-game. That intersection showed no meaningful crowd-size effect, suggesting the parking-and-driving behavior patterns there are less responsive to attendance size than Clark and Irving.

What to do with this information

If the Cubs are playing a rivalry game, a weekend game in peak summer, or have recently gone on a winning streak, expect the traffic around Wrigley to behave like a sold-out house. Plan accordingly: leave earlier, use transit, or park further away and walk.

The LV2 enforcement itself does not change based on crowd size. Officers are in the zone the same way whether 25,000 or 42,000 people are in the park. But the driving conditions around the neighborhood after the game change significantly. A big crowd means more cars competing for the same exits, more aggressive driving at intersections, and longer times to get out of the area.

Transit becomes more attractive the larger the crowd. On sold-out days, the Red Line carries a substantial share of fans and the time advantage over driving and parking can be 30-45 minutes door-to-door for many North Side starting points.
