Rain day Cubs game parking: the data shows a 22% difference

A rainy Cubs game is not great for watching baseball. But for driving and parking in the Wrigley neighborhood, the weather data suggests it is measurably less chaotic than a dry game day.

Published September 17, 2026  ·  Weather + camera data 2025

On Cubs game days with more than 0.1 inches of rain, Clark and Irving red light cameras averaged 12.2 violations per day. On dry game days, the average was 15.7. Rain cuts violations by 22%.

We matched daily precipitation data against red light camera violations at Clark and Irving Park for every 2025 Cubs home game. The rain signal is clear and consistent: wet game days produce fewer traffic violations at this intersection than dry ones.

Why rain reduces violations

Rain changes driver behavior in several measurable ways that all point in the same direction at a busy intersection like Clark and Irving.

First, rain reduces total traffic volume. Some fans who might otherwise drive decide that a rainy Cubs game is a good day to take the Red Line or skip the game entirely. Fewer total cars in the neighborhood means fewer cars competing for the same exit windows at the same lights.

Second, rain slows driving speeds generally. Wet roads increase following distances and reduce the incentive to sprint through a yellow light. The gap between the decision to go and the light being clearly red shrinks on dry roads when drivers are moving faster. On wet roads, drivers tend to approach intersections more conservatively.

Third, rain signals to people that transit and rideshare are reasonable alternatives to their own car. A fan who might normally drive and park two blocks away considers that a car-to-door experience is more appealing in the rain, which pushes them toward Uber or Lyft rather than their own vehicle. This reduces the pool of post-game driver traffic leaving the area at the same time.

What this does not mean

Rain does not suspend LV2 enforcement. Officers are in the zone on rainy game days the same as dry ones. The residential restriction activates at 5 PM regardless of weather. If your car is in the zone without a permit when the zone goes active, it will get a ticket and potentially a tow in the rain exactly as it would on a sunny day.

The 22% reduction in red light violations is a signal about traffic congestion and driver behavior at intersections, not about LV2 enforcement intensity. The two enforcement systems operate independently. Traffic camera violations happen at the intersection. LV2 enforcement happens on the residential streets in the zone. Rain affects the former, not the latter.

The practical takeaway

If you are driving to a rainy Cubs game, the post-game traffic situation is likely to be somewhat better than average. The intersection at Clark and Irving will still be congested, but the data suggests less so than on a dry, warm, sold-out game. The combination of rain plus a big crowd is uncommon. When it happens, the rain effect partially offsets the crowd effect.

Rain days are also better days to experiment with parking further from the stadium and walking in. The neighborhood is typically less saturated with game-day foot traffic on a rainy day, which means more available street parking further out and fewer people competing for it. The walk is unpleasant, but the parking situation may compensate.
