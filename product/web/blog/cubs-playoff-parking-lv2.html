Cubs playoff parking and LV2: what changes, what doesn't.

Since the 2023 ordinance change, LV2 applies to every Cubs home game including postseason. The zone activates the same way it does in June. The crowd that shows up is not the same.

Published September 3, 2026

LV2 runs 5 PM to 10 PM on every Cubs playoff home game, same as regular season. What changes is the crowd size, the arrival timing, the national TV broadcast window, and the share of fans who have never been to the neighborhood before.

What doesn't change

The rule is the rule. LV2 activates at 5:00 PM on any Cubs home game day and enforcement runs until 10:00 PM. This is true for a sold-out World Series Game 7 and a Wednesday afternoon regular season game in May. The zone, the fines, and the enforcement timing are identical.

If you have a valid LV2 permit, it works the same way in October as it does in July. Permits are tied to the zone and the calendar, not to the type of game. The ticket cost structure is the same: $65 base fine, doubled after 25 days unpaid, plus tow and storage if the car is hooked.

The boundaries of the zone do not shift for postseason games. The northern boundary is Irving Park Road, the southern boundary is Addison Street, the eastern edge is Racine Avenue, and the western edge is Ashland Avenue. Same map, same rules.

What does change

Attendance at a postseason home game is almost always a sell-out, often at capacity (roughly 41,000 for Cubs playoff configurations). Regular season averages run closer to 32,000 to 36,000 depending on opponent and weather. The extra 5,000 to 9,000 people means more cars converging on the neighborhood in a compressed window.

The timing of postseason games is controlled by the broadcaster, not the team. Most postseason games are prime-time starts: 7:08 PM for East Coast primetime or 9:08 PM for late-window games. A 9 PM first pitch pushes fan arrival into the 7 to 8:30 PM window, which overlaps with the core of LV2 enforcement. A regular season 7:05 PM game has most fans arriving by 6:30. A 9:08 PM game stretches the arrival window.

Postseason crowds skew toward less experienced Wrigley visitors. Season ticket holders still get their seats, but the secondary market is flooded with first-time buyers, out-of-town fans, and casual Cubs followers who only show up for October baseball. These are the drivers most likely to park in LV2 without a permit because they do not know the zone exists.

The enforcement data from the 2016 and 2017 postseason runs shows ticket volumes per game running 30 to 40 percent higher than a typical regular season night. Not because officers ticket more aggressively, but because more unfamiliar drivers park in the wrong spots.

Security perimeter and road closures

For high-profile postseason games (NLCS, World Series), the city and the Cubs expand the pedestrian perimeter around Wrigley. Clark Street between Addison and Waveland is often closed to vehicle traffic from 4 PM onward. Sheffield between Clark and Kenmore can be restricted. These closures are temporary and specific to that game, announced by the 44th Ward and posted in advance.

The closures do not change LV2. The permit zone is separate from the game-day pedestrian perimeter. You can have a valid LV2 permit and still be unable to drive to your own block because of a World Series road closure. Plan to park earlier in the afternoon or leave your car out of the area for the day.

The permit pressure in October

Demand for LV2 guest passes spikes in the two weeks before postseason games. The 47th Ward's Cubs-specific guest pass is the only way for a non-permit-holder to legally park on a permit street during a game day. Residents who want to host friends or family for playoff games should apply for passes well before the series starts. Last-minute requests during a playoff run are harder to fulfill.

See our guide to LV2 guest passes for Cubs games for the full application process.

The post-game exit is worse

Everything that is bad about a regular season post-game exit is worse in the postseason. The crowd is larger and emotionally charged. National media adds extra vehicle traffic from broadcast and satellite trucks. Bars hold crowds longer because people do not want to leave after a playoff win. Rideshare surge pricing hits its highest levels of the year.

The streets inside and around LV2 typically take 45 to 75 minutes to clear after a playoff home game, compared to 30 to 45 minutes for a regular season night. If you are parked outside the zone and planning to drive home, either leave early or build in time to wait out the exit rush.

Planning for a playoff series

  • Check the LV2 Park homepage before every game day for the exact LV2 status
  • If you are hosting guests, apply for a 47th Ward guest pass before the series begins
  • Expect road closures around the ballpark for NLCS and World Series home games
  • Plan your return trip to leave either before the 7th inning or 45 minutes after the final out
  • Transit (Red Line to Addison) scales better than driving for playoff crowds

The fundamentals of LV2 do not change in October. The stakes for getting it wrong do. A $65 ticket and a $175 tow hurt more when you were also at a playoff game you paid good money to attend.
