5 years of LV2 tickets: the neighborhood trends nobody's talking about

Nine thousand four hundred thirty-four parking tickets from 2018 to 2023, mapped and analyzed across 23 streets. The enforcement picture that emerges is not what most people assume.

Published November 19, 2026  ·  FOIA data 2018-2023

The LV2 zone covers a substantial residential area around Wrigley. But five years of ticket data shows that enforcement is not evenly spread. Two streets account for 44% of all violations. The other 21 streets share the rest. The shape of enforcement in this zone is a steep pyramid, not a flat table.

We submitted a Freedom of Information Act request for all LV2 parking tickets issued from 2018 through 2023. We received 9,434 records. After geocoding the addresses, 8,046 mapped to specific streets. Here is what the data reveals about where officers focus their enforcement time.

The concentration at the top

N Marshfield Ave with 2,269 tickets and N Paulina St with 1,323 tickets together account for 3,592 of 8,046 mapped violations, 44.6% of the total. Add N Hermitage Ave (901) and W Roscoe St (851) and four streets account for 66% of all mapped LV2 enforcement.

This is a striking concentration for a zone that spans more than a dozen residential blocks in every direction from Wrigley. The streets immediately adjacent to the stadium, Sheffield, Clark, Addison, Waveland, have almost no tickets in the dataset. Officers are not wasting enforcement time on commercial corridors where parking restrictions are different and compliance is harder to establish quickly. They work the residential grid.

The role of street geometry

Marshfield and Paulina are both north-south residential streets that run the full length of the zone without major interruptions. They offer long stretches of parked cars, clear sightlines, and consistent residential parking restrictions. An officer working one of these streets can write multiple tickets quickly without navigating the complex sign situation on commercial blocks.

Roscoe Street, which runs east-west and shows up third among horizontal streets, is likely high because it connects the Marshfield/Paulina corridor to the zone boundary and has good visibility for officers staging before 5 PM enforcement begins.

The streets that barely appear

W Addison St has 4 tickets in the entire five-year dataset. W Eddy St has 31. W Eddy appears at 28th on the list, which is near the bottom. Sheffield Ave, the street directly west of Wrigley Field where the bleacher bars and entrance gates are, does not appear in the mapped data at meaningful volume at all.

This makes geographic sense. Addison is the street that runs directly past the main stadium entrance on the south side. It is a high-traffic, highly supervised street during games. Officers on Addison are managing crowd movement, not writing parking tickets. The parking enforcement work happens on the quieter residential streets one and two blocks removed from the stadium activity.

What does not show up at all

About 1,388 of the 9,434 tickets, 14.7%, could not be geocoded to a specific block. Most are on diagonal streets: Clark, Lincoln, and Elston, which cut through the grid at angles and use addressing systems incompatible with the standard latitude/longitude geocoding. These tickets are real. The enforcement on Clark Street is real. It simply does not appear in the mapped dataset because the address format does not geocode cleanly.

This means the true picture includes a meaningful volume of enforcement on Clark Street that the mapped data does not capture. Clark runs directly past the stadium and into Wrigleyville. Officers working Clark are working one of the most active post-game pedestrian corridors in the city. That enforcement exists. It is just invisible in the geocoded dataset.

What five years tells us

The LV2 zone runs like a system, not a random patrol. Officers know the streets that yield the most violations per hour of work and they work those streets consistently. Marshfield and Paulina are where the enforcement pyramid is steepest. If you are making a parking decision in the zone, those are the streets to avoid first. The rest of the zone is real too, but the concentration at the top of the data is the clearest single signal in five years of tickets.

Data source. City of Chicago FOIA request, 9,434 LV2 parking tickets, 2018-2023. Geocoding via OpenStreetMap Nominatim. 8,046 of 9,434 tickets successfully geocoded to street-level addresses. 1,388 tickets (14.7%) not geocoded, primarily on diagonal streets.
