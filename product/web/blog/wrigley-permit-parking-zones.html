Every permit parking zone within 10 blocks of Wrigley, explained.

LV2 is the one people know. It is also only one of several overlapping permit restrictions around Wrigley Field. Here's how to read the signs and understand what each zone means for your car.

Published September 10, 2026

The Wrigley area has two layers of permit parking. Standard residential permit zones run all day or specific hours. LV2 is a separate game-day overlay that activates on Cubs home days. A block can be both a residential permit zone and part of LV2, which means it has two sets of restrictions stacked on top of each other.

How Chicago permit parking works

Chicago has more than 1,800 residential permit parking zones citywide. Each zone covers a specific block group, usually a few streets in a neighborhood. Zones are created by the City Council at the request of a ward office, typically after residents petition for relief from non-resident parking pressure.

A standard residential permit zone restricts parking to permit holders during specific hours. The most common pattern is 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, with a grace period for visitors (usually two hours). Some zones are more limited: weekday evenings only, or specific seasonal months.

The permit itself is tied to a street address inside the zone. You apply through your alderman's office or the city clerk. The sticker goes on the car's windshield or bumper. One permit covers one car. Second and third cars at the same household can get additional permits at higher cost.

The two layers near Wrigley

Layer 1: Standard residential permit zones

The streets around Wrigley (parts of Lakeview, Wrigleyville, Roscoe Village, Boystown, Uptown, and Buena Park) are covered by numbered residential permit zones. Most are 24/7. If you park on one of these blocks without a permit, you can be ticketed at any time of day or night, game or no game.

These zones predate LV2 and operate year-round. They cover the same residents whose blocks are inside the LV2 boundary, plus residents in adjacent blocks where LV2 does not apply.

Layer 2: LV2 (the Cubs game-day zone)

LV2 is a separate special restriction that applies only on Cubs home game days, 5 PM to 10 PM. It covers a specific rectangle bounded by Irving Park (north), Addison (south), Racine (east), and Ashland (west). Inside that rectangle, only cars with LV2-specific permits or guest passes can park during game-day enforcement hours.

If a block is inside the LV2 boundary AND inside a standard residential permit zone, the car needs both permits: the standard residential permit for regular hours, and an LV2 permit for game days. The two permits are tied to the same address but are issued separately.

A common mistake: assuming an LV2 permit gets you into any residential permit zone in the area. It does not. LV2 is only valid inside the LV2 boundary, only during game-day enforcement windows. A standard residential permit is needed for all other times and blocks.

How to read the signs on your block

Signs are posted at the start and end of each zone and at regular intervals along the block. A typical Wrigley-area block will have stacked signs: one sign for the residential permit zone (naming the zone number), one for LV2 (with the zone letters and hours), and often a street cleaning sign at the curb.

Read top to bottom. The signs are listed in order of specificity: most permanent at the top, most temporary at the bottom. A car has to comply with every sign that applies. If any one of them prohibits parking at the current moment, the car can be ticketed.

Finding your exact zone

The City of Chicago publishes an interactive permit zone map at cityofchicago.org. Enter a street address and it returns the zone number, the permit type, and the contact information for the ward office that administers the zone.

For LV2 specifically, the 44th Ward and 47th Ward split the administration. Addresses south of the 44th/47th ward line apply through the 44th Ward. Addresses north of the line apply through the 47th Ward. The ward boundary shifts block by block, so verify your ward before applying.

What happens when you get it wrong

A standard residential permit zone ticket runs $65. If the block is also inside LV2 and you get ticketed during a game-day enforcement window, the fine is still $65, but the risk of a tow is significantly higher. Officers on active LV2 enforcement will hook cars that are in the zone without proper credentials, not just ticket them.

If the car is towed, the total cost reaches $275 to $310 depending on how quickly you retrieve it. See our breakdown of what an LV2 ticket actually costs for the full fee stack.

The short answer for visitors

If you are visiting someone inside the LV2 boundary on a game day, do not park on their block unless they have a guest pass for you. If you are visiting someone outside the LV2 boundary but still inside a residential permit zone, you can park during any grace period or during posted visitor-allowed hours, but you need a permit or pass for longer stays. If you do not have a guest pass and you are inside any permit zone during its enforcement window, expect a ticket.

When in doubt, park outside the permit zones entirely. Streets east of Ashland and north of Irving Park, or streets west of Southport, generally have blocks without permit restrictions where visitors can park freely on non-game days.
