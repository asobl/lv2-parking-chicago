Can you park on Clark Street near Wrigley Field?

Short answer: mostly no. Clark Street near Wrigley is a commercial corridor, a CTA bus route, a pedestrian zone on game nights, and a near-constant no-parking artery. The few legal parking spots are short-term metered spaces that fill within minutes of opening.

Published October 15, 2026

Clark Street between Irving Park and Belmont is not a parking strategy. It is a through-traffic corridor with commercial loading, bus stops, and event-day restrictions. The few legal spots turn over fast and are not reliable for a multi-hour game-day stay.

Why Clark Street is different

Clark is a major arterial running diagonally northwest through the north side. It passes directly alongside Wrigley Field, meets Addison at the ballpark entrance, and carries thousands of pedestrians, CTA #22 buses, delivery vans, and rideshare pickups every game day. The city's curb usage reflects all of that: most of the block face is dedicated to moving vehicles and people, not storing cars.

On a game day, the curb is further carved up by event-day restrictions. Sections that would be legal parking on a random Tuesday become no-parking zones from mid-afternoon through two hours after the final out. The rules change faster here than almost anywhere else in the city.

The meters that exist

There are metered spaces along Clark, but they are designed for short-term commercial visits, not game-day parking. Typical meter hours run 8 AM to 10 PM. Rates are standard Chicago meter rates. Maximum stay is usually two hours, and enforcement on Clark is aggressive because the meters are managed by the same system that runs the Loop and north side commercial corridors.

The meters accept ParkChicago app payment. You can extend remotely, but only up to the posted maximum stay. If the maximum is two hours, you cannot pay for three. A game lasts three hours minimum plus warmup plus post-game. Meter parking does not cover a game.

If you pay the meter for two hours and the game goes long, you get a ticket when the meter expires. The ticket is $65 and parking enforcement on Clark during game days is immediate.

Game-day restrictions that show up on Clark

  • Event no-parking signs: Posted in advance for specific games. The signs are placed along the curb and say "No parking this side of street [date] [time]" with a time window that often starts in the early afternoon.
  • Pedestrian perimeter: For NLCS, World Series, and other high-security games, Clark between Addison and Waveland is closed to vehicle traffic entirely. No stopping, no standing, no parking.
  • Rideshare pickup zones: Temporary signs designate sections of the curb as rideshare drop-off and pickup on game days. Private vehicles parked in these zones are ticketed.
  • Emergency vehicle access: Fire lane restrictions are enforced year-round but get stricter on game days when fire department resources are pre-staged.

Cross-streets off Clark

The cross-streets that intersect Clark (Waveland, Addison, Cornelia, Roscoe, School, Belmont) have their own mix of LV2 zone coverage, residential permits, and street cleaning windows. Some blocks are inside LV2, some are not, depending on exactly where they fall relative to the Racine boundary.

For the cross-streets inside LV2, the same game-day 5 PM restriction applies. For cross-streets outside LV2 but inside residential permit zones, the standard permit requirement applies year-round. Very few blocks in this area are unrestricted.

What actually works on Clark

Three scenarios where Clark parking is a legitimate choice:

  • Quick errand, not a game: Pre-game visits to a Clark Street business (dinner, shopping) using a two-hour meter are fine, as long as you move the car before the event no-parking window starts.
  • Post-10 PM parking: After the LV2 window ends and meters expire, overnight parking on Clark is legal in metered zones that say "free after 10 PM" or unmetered. This is not useful for a game-day arrival but can work for post-game dinner or drinks in the area.
  • Commercial visits off-peak: Morning or mid-afternoon visits on non-game days are straightforward. Meter, pay, park, leave within the posted window.

What does not work on Clark

  • Any plan that involves leaving a car on Clark for the duration of a Cubs game
  • Parking on Clark during a posted event no-parking window, under any circumstances
  • Parking in a loading zone outside posted business hours (these are enforced on game days)
  • Stopping in a rideshare pickup zone "just for a minute"
  • Any block of Clark between Addison and Waveland during a high-security game

The useful heuristic: if you can see Wrigley from your parking spot on Clark, do not park there on a game day. If you cannot see Wrigley and the meter allows the time you need, read every sign before you walk away.
