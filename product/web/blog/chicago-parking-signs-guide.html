Chicago parking signs explained: every type, what it means, and what it costs to ignore it.

Chicago has a lot of different parking signs and stacking multiple signs on one post is common. Here's a plain-English guide to every sign type you'll encounter.

Multiple signs stacked on one post all apply simultaneously. Read every sign on the post before parking. "I only saw one sign" is not a defense.

Permit parking signs

Permit Parking Only
Tow Zone
The most common type. Shows the zone number, the hours of restriction, and sometimes specific event triggers (like "Cubs Night Games"). Only vehicles with a valid permit for that zone may park during the posted hours. Anyone else gets ticketed and towed.
Example: "Permit Parking / No Parking 6 PM–6 AM Except With Permit 383 / Cubs Night Games / Tow Zone 5–10 PM" — Zone 383 permit holders can park during night game hours; everyone else is towed from 5–10 PM on game days, and all non-residents are restricted 6 PM–6 AM year-round.
Violation: ticket + tow. Retrieval from Auto Pound #6 costs $160–$200 minimum.
Chicago Municipal Code 9-64-090 (permit parking); 9-68-023 (Wrigley/LV2 special event zone)
LV2 Permit Zone
Special Event Tow Zone
A subset of permit parking specific to the Wrigley Field area. LV2 only activates on Cubs home game dates (including postseason) and Wrigley concert dates, 5 PM–10 PM. Unlike standard permit zones, LV2 is not active every day — only on event days. Permits are free and issued by your ward alderman's office.
Violation on a game/concert day after 5 PM: ticket + immediate tow.
Chicago Municipal Code 9-68-023

Street cleaning signs

Street Cleaning — Tow Zone
Tow Zone (seasonal)
Posted on streets where the city runs mechanical street sweepers. The sign specifies the day(s) and time(s) when parking is prohibited so the sweeper can pass. Common format: "Street Cleaning / 9 AM–3 PM / 3rd Monday of Month / Apr 1–Nov 30 / Weather Permitting / Tow Zone."
Read carefully: "3rd Monday" means it's only the third Monday of each month, not every Monday. "Weather permitting" means sweeping is cancelled if it rained, but the tow zone still applies unless there's an official cancellation.
Violation: ticket ($60–$100) + tow during active sweeping hours. The sweeper doesn't tow — parking enforcement officers ahead of the sweeper do.
Chicago Municipal Code 10-8-330

Snow parking signs

Snow Route / Winter Overnight Ban
Seasonal Tow Zone
The blue snowflake sign. Reads: "No Parking 3 AM–7 AM / Dec 1–Apr 1 / Or When Snow Is Over 2 Inches / Tow Zone." There are two separate triggers: (1) the calendar dates Dec 1–Apr 1 mean no overnight parking from 3–7 AM regardless of weather, and (2) a snow emergency declaration activates restrictions even outside those hours when accumulation exceeds 2 inches.
Snow emergencies are declared by the city and announced via Chicago's website, 311, and local news. During a declared emergency, the restriction applies to ALL streets with the snowflake sign.
Violation: ticket + tow. Getting towed during a snowstorm and retrieving your car is especially miserable — plan ahead.
Chicago Municipal Code 9-40-060 (winter overnight ban); snow emergency declarations under Chapter 2-8

Loading and standing zones

Loading Zone / Standing Zone
No Parking
Two related but different restrictions. A Loading Zone (usually red and white) prohibits parking and standing except for active loading/unloading of goods. A Standing Zone allows a vehicle to stop and wait (with the driver present) but prohibits parking. Both are typically posted with time restrictions showing when they apply.
If you park in a loading zone without actively loading, even briefly, it's a violation. "I was only in there for two minutes" is not a defense — the restriction is about what you're doing, not how long you're there.
Violation: ticket. May be towed if you're blocking access to a loading dock or fire lane.
Commercial Loading Zone — 24 Hour Paid Zone
Paid Zone
Near business districts, some loading zones require payment via ParkChicago to use. Signs read: "Commercial Loading Zone / 24 Hour Paid Zone / Pay at Pay Box." You pay at the meter or via the ParkChicago app and your vehicle can then legally occupy the space for loading. Without payment, it's a violation even if you're actively loading.
Chicago Municipal Code 9-68-040

Pay to park signs

Pay at Pay Box / ParkChicago
Metered Parking
Chicago's current metered parking system uses pay boxes (kiosks at the end of the block) rather than individual meters on each space. The sign shows the zone number and directs you to "Sign up at ParkChicago.com for mobile pay." You can pay at the kiosk or through the ParkChicago app. The app lets you extend your session remotely without walking back to the kiosk.
Hours and rates vary by location and day. Downtown meters run 7 days a week. Many neighborhood meters are free on Sundays. Check the kiosk or ParkChicago app for current rates.
Violation: ticket. Vehicles may be booted after two unpaid tickets.

Accessibility / disability parking

Accessible Parking (Blue Wheelchair Symbol)
Disability Permit Required
Reserved for vehicles displaying a valid Illinois disability placard or disabled veteran license plate. These spaces are on the street where a property owner has requested them, or in designated accessible spots. No time limit applies to vehicles with a valid placard in these spaces (though you still can't park during street cleaning or snow emergencies). Parking in an accessible space without a valid placard is one of the highest-fined violations in Chicago.
Violation: $250 base fine. Tow is common.
Chicago Municipal Code 9-64-170; Illinois Vehicle Code 11-1301.3

How to read stacked signs

When multiple signs are stacked on the same post, they all apply at their respective times. You have to mentally layer them:

  • Read each sign individually for its hours and conditions
  • The most restrictive rule at any given time is the one that applies
  • Arrow signs below a restriction indicate which direction on the block the rule covers (sometimes a rule only covers part of a block)
  • When in doubt: don't park there. One ticket pays for a lot of parking garage visits.

Near Wrigley Field, a single post can have a Zone 383 permit sign, an LV2 event restriction, and a street cleaning sign stacked together. Each applies during its own window. All three can apply to the same space on the same day.
