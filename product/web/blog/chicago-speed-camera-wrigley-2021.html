How one policy change made the speed camera near Wrigley go 4x overnight

In March 2021, Chicago changed one number. A camera on Irving Park Road near Wrigley Field went from 19,000 violations a year to 75,000 in twelve months. Irving Park did not get more dangerous. The threshold got lower.

Published August 20, 2026  ·  Camera data 2014-2025

The speed camera at 1142 W Irving Park, one block east of Wrigley Field, recorded 19,064 violations in 2019. In 2021, the same camera recorded 75,077. Nothing changed about the street, the speed limit, or how people drove. Chicago lowered the minimum ticketable speed from 10 mph over the limit to 6 mph over.

This is the most striking finding in twelve years of camera data near Wrigley Field. It is not about the Cubs. It is not about game days. It is about a policy decision that turned a mid-volume school zone camera into one of the highest-output enforcement cameras on Chicago's North Side in a single calendar year.

What changed and when

Chicago's speed camera program was launched in 2013 in school and park zones. From the start, the enforcement threshold was set at 11 mph over the posted speed limit, later adjusted to 10 mph over. Under this threshold, a driver going 41 in a 30 mph zone would get a ticket. A driver going 39 mph would not.

In March 2021, citing safety concerns and a desire to slow drivers in school zones, the Chicago City Council approved lowering the enforcement threshold to 6 mph over the limit. A driver going 36 in a 30 mph zone would now receive a ticket. The change took effect immediately for all active school zone cameras.

The math on this is straightforward. At 10 mph over, only the fastest drivers in the camera's range get flagged. At 6 mph over, a much larger portion of the normal speed distribution crosses the threshold. For a camera on a busy arterial like Irving Park, the effect was immediate and enormous.

CHI095 at 1142 W Irving Park sits in a school zone on a four-lane arterial that runs east-west across the North Side. It is active during school hours, roughly 7 AM to 4 PM on school days. It has no relationship to Cubs game enforcement, which runs after school hours. But its location on a major street one block from Wrigley means it captures high volumes of North Side traffic all day long.

What happened after the spike

The 75,077-violation year of 2021 was not sustained. From that peak, violations declined every subsequent year: 57,283 in 2022, 43,867 in 2023, 37,249 in 2024, and roughly 35,824 through early 2025. That is a 52% decline from peak in four years.

The most straightforward explanation is driver adaptation. Drivers who regularly use Irving Park near Wrigley learned where the camera is and adjusted their speed at that specific point. Whether that represents genuinely slower driving through the school zone or just foot-off-the-gas at the camera location is a different question that the violation data alone cannot answer.

The decline pattern is consistent with what researchers have documented at speed cameras more broadly: an initial spike when a new threshold takes effect, followed by a gradual decline as local drivers adapt. The spike is real. The adaptation is also real.

Why this matters beyond Wrigley

The CHI095 pattern is the same pattern that played out at speed cameras across Chicago in 2021. The Wrigley-area camera is notable because of its location and because the data is easy to contextualize. But the policy decision affected every school zone camera in the city simultaneously.

For drivers in Chicago who wonder why their camera violation count seems high relative to how they drive, the 2021 threshold change is the structural answer. The behavior that was legal in 2019 at 38 mph in a 30 zone became illegal in 2021. The cameras did not get more aggressive. The definition of violation got broader.
