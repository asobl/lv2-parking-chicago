Title: I had 9,434 parking tickets sitting on my laptop and did nothing with them for two years
~650 words
I had 9,434 parking tickets sitting on my laptop and did nothing with them for two years. In 2023, I filed a FOIA request with the City of Chicago asking for every LV2 parking ticket issued near Wrigley Field. The city sent me a spreadsheet. 9,434 tickets. 2018 to 2023. Every address, every fine, every date. I put it in a folder and forgot about it. Fast forward to this spring. A friend texted me before a Cubs game: "Is LV2 active today or can I just park there?" I didn't know. I looked it up manually. It took ten minutes and three different sites. The answer was obvious once I found it, but finding it was annoying enough that I thought: someone should build a thing that just answers this question. Then I remembered the spreadsheet. --- A little context if you don't live in Chicago. The LV2 zone is the residential parking enforcement zone around Wrigley Field. On game days and event days, you need a permit. Park without one and you get a ticket. The zone has been around for years, but there's no single place that tells you whether enforcement is active today, right now, before you drive there. Most people either know someone who knows, or they guess, or they circle the block looking for street sweeping signs and hoping. 9,434 tickets worth of people guessed wrong. --- I spent a weekend pulling the data out of the folder, building a map with it, and connecting it to the Cubs and Ticketmaster schedules so the site would update automatically. The site is called LV2 Park. It lives at lv2park.com. It does one thing: tells you if LV2 parking enforcement is active today, yes or no. It also shows the week ahead, a heat map of where tickets cluster by street (the blocks west of Ashland got hammered -- Marshfield alone had 2,269 tickets over five years), and a full schedule so you know which game days to avoid. The whole thing runs automatically. Four times a day it checks whether there's a game, whether events are scheduled, whether you should park there or not. No manual updates. I don't have to touch it. --- Here's what I keep coming back to. I had the data in 2023. The question my friend asked has been asked thousands of times. Every Cubs fan, every Lakeview resident who parks on the street, every person who moved to Roscoe Village and had no idea the zone existed until they got a $65 ticket on a Tuesday afternoon in October. The question has always been there. I just sat on the answer for two years. This is the part of building I try to be honest about: ideas are not the bottleneck. I have a hard drive full of data, notes, half-built tools, and FOIA requests that never became anything. The bottleneck is the thing that makes you actually finish. For me it was a friend asking a simple question. Go check it out: lv2park.com. And if you've gotten an LV2 ticket in the past six years, your address is probably on the heat map.
