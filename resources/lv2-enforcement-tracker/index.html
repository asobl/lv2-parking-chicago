LV2 Enforcement Tracker: 5 Seasons of Data

Does LV2 enforcement actually happen every game? We tracked 9,434 tickets across five Cubs seasons using FOIA records. The answer: yes, nearly every time.

The Core Finding

LV2 enforcement is not random and not occasional. Across five seasons of FOIA data, tickets were issued on virtually every Cubs home game night that the zone was active. The 2020 COVID season (no fans in attendance, shortened to 60 games) shows near-zero tickets. That one outlier confirms the pattern: enforcement is tied directly to game attendance, not the schedule alone.

When the Cubs play and fans show up, LV2 officers show up. If you're parked in the zone after 5 PM on a game day without a permit, you are almost certainly getting a ticket.

Ticket Volume by Season

Ticket counts varied year to year based on attendance, game count, and enforcement staffing. The 2020 season is clearly the outlier. 2021 and 2022 show the highest per-game rates in the dataset, consistent with pent-up demand bringing larger and less-experienced crowds back after COVID.

What the Tickets Are Tagged As

The FOIA records include a tag field indicating what type of event, if any, was associated with each ticket. Game-tagged tickets make up 39% of the total. But the large "untagged" category doesn't mean those tickets weren't game-related — it means the enforcement system didn't explicitly annotate them to an event. Most untagged tickets in this dataset fall on days the Cubs were at home.

Enforcement Starts at Exactly 5:00 PM

The FOIA data shows a sharp timing pattern. Ticket issuance is effectively zero before 5:00 PM on game days. At 5:00 PM, it spikes. By 5:05 PM, officers are issuing 60 to 70 tickets per minute across the zone.

This is not a gradual build-up. It's a coordinated deployment. Multiple officers enter the zone simultaneously at 5 PM and work through their assigned streets. The first 15 minutes are the peak enforcement window.

Rule change — 2023 season

LV2 Now Applies to ALL Home Games

Before the 2023 season, LV2 enforcement was limited to night games. Starting in 2023, the City of Chicago expanded enforcement to every Cubs home game — day games included. A 1:20 PM game now triggers LV2 enforcement from 5 PM onward, even if the game ends by 3:30 PM. If you park before the game and don't return until after 5 PM, you will get ticketed.

This rule change significantly increases the risk for fans who attend afternoon games and plan to stay in the area for drinks or dinner after. Plan accordingly.

Streets With the Most Tickets

Enforcement is concentrated in specific corridors. The top five streets by ticket count in the FOIA data account for the majority of all LV2 tickets. These are not the streets immediately next to Wrigley (those are often metered or no-parking) but the residential streets 3 to 8 blocks away where fans think they've found safe street parking.

How to Read This as a Parking Decision

  • Enforcement is near-certain on game days. Across 5 seasons, there is no recorded game night without LV2 enforcement. Plan accordingly.
  • The zone activates at 5:00 PM exactly. If your game ends at 3:30 PM, your car is safe if you retrieve it before 5:00. If you stay for food or drinks, it is not.
  • Day games carry the same risk as night games since 2023. The rule change that season ended the night-game-only window.
  • Marshfield and Paulina are the highest-risk streets. Fans park there assuming they're far enough west. They're not. Those streets are inside the zone.
  • Concerts are enforced too. 577 concert-tagged tickets in the dataset. If there's a concert at Wrigley, treat it exactly like a game.
